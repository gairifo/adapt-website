The problem with staying

Every year, more COBOL developers retire and fewer enter the field. Your bus factor shrinks. Mainframe licensing — MIPS, MSU, storage — only goes up. And bolting modern capabilities onto a mainframe-centric architecture means more middleware, more latency, more fragility.

→

The problem with manual rewrites

COBOL mainframe estates are among the hardest to rewrite manually. Decades of business logic are encoded in programs, copybooks, and job control language that no single person fully understands. Manual rewrites take 3–7 years, cost tens of millions, and most fail — 74% of mainframe modernization projects stall or are abandoned.

Inside the COBOL factory

AI parses your COBOL programs and copybooks. Architects own the structure.

01

COBOL parsing

Our AI ingests your COBOL programs, copybooks, JCL, CICS BMS maps, DB2 schemas, and VSAM file definitions. It builds a complete map of every program, paragraph, data structure, transaction, and batch job. Decades of implicit knowledge become explicit and inspectable.

02

Target generation

The AI emits idiomatic code in your chosen target stack — Java/Spring, .NET, Node.js, or Python. COBOL data structures become modern domain models. Batch jobs become scheduled services. CICS transactions become APIs. Senior architects review every structural decision.

03

Equivalence testing

Before any cutover, the AI synthesizes behavioral tests comparing new system output against mainframe output — transaction by transaction, batch job by batch job. You migrate on a passing diff, not on faith.
